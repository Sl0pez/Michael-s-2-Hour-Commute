Michael's 2 Hour Commute

To a school 12 minutes away

By Santiago Lopez

May 10, 2018

Every day Michael wakes up at 5:30 in the morning, crying. He would rather be in bed. But, that’s the price to pay for early intervention. Michael is a 3-year-old boy with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD). Early intervention treats symptoms of ASD and helps him learn how to function independently. The family is forced to choose between keeping him home and sending him on a daily commute that can top five and a half hours a day. If he were alone on the bus, the ride would take just 11 minutes, door to door.

Michael lives in Ditmars, 20 minutes or so from his school, NYL Forest Hills West, a preschool for children 2-5 years old. His family moved to the US from the Philippines 3 years ago when his dad got a job offer with better pay. The US also happens to have better services for people like Michael than the Philippines.

The move has been hard on the young family because they don’t have family and friends with them. “It’s a lot harder because we don’t have yayas (maids) and family here,” said Michael’s mother referring to the fact that their family is still in the Philippines. “We have to do everything ourselves which is why I stay home to look after the babies and do all the house chores.”

Michael’s family wishes to remain anonymous because they don’t want their son to be the subject of retaliation from his school, the Office of Pupil Transportation (OPT), the agency that coordinates school bus assignments and routes for the city's Education Department, or the private bus company contracted by the city. The want their son to be referred to only as “Michael.” This is his story.

Michael's commute to school if he drove


Michael’s mom loves his school. “The teachers are great, [Michael] has an occupational therapist, a speech therapist, and he’s going to go into PT (physical therapy) soon. He needs all the help he can get, and the schools fill up pretty fast. [The classes] have 8 people, a teacher, and 3 assistants so my son is really taken care of.”

His mom says that without traffic, the car ride to his school can be as short as 11 minutes but because he is the first on his bus route, he has to endure a 2-hour long commute to school every day.

How long it takes to get to Michael's school from every community district in NYC


When Michael started school in February, his mom says that the bus would come around as early as 6:00 am to bring him to his school where classes start around 8:20 am.

“There was one time the heater was on his face and he got a nosebleed and he was in the bus for too long,” says Michael’s mom. “I called [the bus company] and they told me that the manager would speak to me and call me back and they asked for my number, but I haven’t gotten a call back.”

In the afternoon, he leaves school at 2 pm and arrives home between 3 and 3:30 pm. An hour and a half commute is something Michael’s family is okay with.

However, his pick-up time has gradually decreased to 6:30 am and on good days, to 7:00 am as a result of his family’s constant calls to the OPT and through coordination with the bus company, Alina Services Corp, through their school. Officials from the OPT and Alina declined requests for comment.

His mom says that they call the OPT every day, hoping for a solution to Michael’s long commute.

The bus situation is frustrating for the family because they have no other means of transportation for Michael to get to school. Michael’s dad drives to work at 7:30 am every day which means that if he drops Michael off on the way to work, Michael would be at the school before it opened. Michael’s mom has yet to learn how to drive and needs to stay home with his younger sibling.

Michael’s bus situation is not out of the norm, according to Sara Catalinotto of Parents to Improve School Transportation (PIST). “It’s not really a person-centered kind of a system, it’s being programmed the same way you might do any kind of logistics for getting packages delivered from one point to another and that’s not what our kids need,” she said.

According to Catalinotto, there is a policy in place where a bus ride within the borough should only be 90 minutes in total, accounting for 15 minutes in traffic. The total limit for a bus ride also decreases depending on the age of the students. However, based on reports they have uncovered (with the students’ names redacted) the PIST have found that most buses fill up the 90 minutes with multiple stops to houses and even other schools that don’t necessarily have the same dismissal times.

“It’s almost 3 hours on the bus?” exclaims Michael’s mom. “It’s not acceptable!”
